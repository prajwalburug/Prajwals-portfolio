**The result is the same every time:** the data shows that unstructured interviews predict job performance only marginally better than chance. And the candidates who suffer most are the ones whose backgrounds don't match the interviewer's mental model of a 'good hire.'

## What Most Teams Get Wrong About Interview Fairness

Most teams think fairness means 'being nice to everyone.' That's not how bias works.

Bias in hiring is structural. It lives in the process, not the people. When questions change from candidate to candidate, when scoring is left to intuition, when interviewers aren't calibrated against each other — the result isn't fairness. It's noise dressed as judgment.

**Here's what the research actually says:**
• Structured interviews are 2x more predictive of job performance than unstructured ones
• The same interviewers evaluating the same candidates produce different scores depending on time of day, mood, and candidate order
• Removing personal identifiers from evaluations eliminates the single largest source of unconscious bias

Fairness isn't a feeling. It's a design choice.

The teams that get this right share a common pattern. They invest in the process first — the scoring criteria, the question design, the evaluation workflow — and let the technology amplify that foundation. Technology without process is just automation. Process with technology is transformation.

## How Structured Evaluation Removes Bias from the Equation

Structured evaluation works because it removes variability. The same questions, the same scoring rubric, the same evaluation criteria — applied to every candidate, every time.

Here's how that plays out in practice with an AI voice interview:

1. **Every candidate gets the same starting questions**, tied to the competencies the role requires
2. **AI probes answers in real time**, consistent follow-up logic applied based on response patterns
3. **Scoring is deterministic** — the same answer gets the same score, regardless of who gives it or when
4. **Evaluation happens without PII** — name, age, location, and other demographic markers are stripped before assessment

Hiring managers spend 12 hours scheduling interviews for every 1 hour evaluating candidates. This isn't an edge case. It's the norm. And it's fixable.

## Building a PII-Safe Evaluation Pipeline

Candidate privacy and evaluation fairness are the same problem.

When an AI model processes a resume that includes a candidate's name, the model can infer gender, ethnicity, and approximate age. Even if the model is 'blind' to those attributes, the statistical correlations in the training data create proxy signals.

**The fix is architectural:** strip PII before any AI processing touches the candidate's data. At that point, the model evaluates only what the candidate produced — their answers, their skills, their fit against the role criteria.

This isn't just a compliance checkbox. It's the difference between an AI that amplifies bias and an AI that eliminates it.
